# Atmos Vertical Page: Modular Template Architecture

A research-backed module structure for industry-specific landing pages. Built from the Digital Applied 2,000-page A/B study (Q4 2025 to Q1 2026), Unbounce 2025 Conversion Benchmark Report (57M conversions), First Page Sage industrial benchmarks, Genesys Growth, Lovable industrial design study, Directive Consulting, and Surface Labs 2025 B2B lead benchmarks.

---

## Conversion expectations (set the baseline)

Industrial equipment sits at 1.5 to 2.2% on lead-gen pages (First Page Sage 2025). That is below the 2.9% general B2B average and the 3.8% B2B SaaS median.

Demo and contact-form pages average 1 to 3.5%, with top performers hitting 6 to 8% (grow-conversions.com 2026; Surface Labs 2025).

Lead-gen/quote pages (the closest match to Atmos) sit at a 6.8% median across the Digital Applied study (n=320).

Realistic Atmos target: 3 to 5% on paid-traffic vertical pages. Stretch target: 6 to 8%. If you clear 4% on cold paid traffic into a $20K+ equipment quote, you are operating above the top quartile for industrial.

---

## Architecture principles (10 findings that hold across every module)

1. **Specificity beats vagueness at every layer.** Named-customer count beats aggregate stats (+22% vs +9%). Industry-specific testimonials beat generic ones. Real vertical environments beat staged stock.

2. **Subtraction beats addition.** Multi-CTA heroes (3+ buttons) lose 8%. Forms past 4 fields roughly halve conversion every step. Stock-photo heroes lose 11% versus the control.

3. **Sticky-bottom CTA is the single highest-leverage placement.** Sticky alone = +11% lift, sticky + above-fold = +12%. The two do not compound past +1%, so you are buying placement insurance, not multiplied lift.

4. **Named social proof is the single highest-leverage trust pattern.** "Trusted by 8 of the Fortune 50" beats logo strips, aggregate stats, press logos, and star ratings.

5. **Mobile is non-negotiable.** 68% of B2B research happens on mobile (Instapage 2026). Mobile drives 40%+ of B2B revenue in leading orgs (Genesys 2026).

6. **<2s LCP is the cliff edge.** Pages under 1s convert at 4.4%, 2 to 3s at 3.6%, 4s+ at 1.7%. Every second past 2 costs roughly 1 conversion point.

7. **Single primary CTA matched to buying mode.** "Get a quote" beats "Contact us" on services by 14%. Single-CTA pages hit 13.5% vs 10.5% for multi-CTA (GrowthSpree 2026).

8. **Trust signals belong adjacent to the CTA, not on the About page.** Placement near CTAs drives 34 to 42% conversion lifts (Surface Labs 2025).

9. **Progressive disclosure for complex products.** B2B equipment buying involves 6 to 10 stakeholders over 3 to 18 months (Gartner 2024). The page has to serve operators, owners, vets, trainers, and finance simultaneously.

10. **Distributed trust beats one stacked block.** Spread testimonials, certifications, and named-customer counts across the page near each decision point, not in one social-proof section.

---

## The 10-module structure

Each module documents: what it does, why it works (with research citation), what changes per vertical, what stays constant, and Atmos-specific copy direction.

---

### Module 1: Hero

**Job:** Communicate relevance, value, and next-step in under 3 seconds. 53% of visitors leave within 3 seconds if relevance is unclear (GrowthSpree 2026).

**Pattern:** Single-stat or single-outcome H1. The Digital Applied study tested 12 hero variants. The single-stat hero (one giant number as H1) won at +18% vs control. Customer-quote hero came second at +12%. Generic stock photography hero lost 11%, the worst result in the study. Video autoplay heroes lost 7% from LCP penalty (2.4s vs 1.3s for image).

**Structure:**
- Single-stat or single-outcome H1, under 8 words and 44 characters (SaaSHero 2026 standard)
- Subhead: who it serves, what they get, in one sentence
- Single primary CTA: "Book a demo" or "Get a quote" (match to buying mode)
- Hero image: real vertical environment, not stock
- No video autoplay
- Mobile-first split-screen on desktop, stacked on mobile

**Per vertical (variable):**
- H1 stat or outcome anchored to the vertical's economics
- Hero image: equine facility, clinic treatment room, sports recovery floor, medspa interior
- Subhead language

**Constant:**
- Layout, type system, CTA button style, CTA verb logic

**Atmos copy direction (no em dashes, loss-framed, numbers over adjectives):**
- Equine: "Cut recovery time for 40+ horses per facility." Subhead: "Localized cryotherapy your vet team can run in-house, with training and support included."
- Clinics: "Add a $4K to $12K monthly revenue line your patients already want." Subhead: "Localized cryotherapy, training, and ongoing support. One vendor."
- Sports Recovery: "Recover athletes in 12 minutes, on-site." Subhead: "Equipment, protocols, and staff training. Built for recovery floors."
- Wellness/Medspa: "Add localized cryotherapy without adding headcount." Subhead: "Equipment, training, and support. One contract."

### Module 2: Named social proof bar

**Job:** Validate within view of the hero. 76% of high-converting B2B demo pages place client logos immediately below the headline (Genesys 2026).

**Pattern:** Named-customer count above the fold. The Digital Applied study found named-customer counts ("Trusted by 8 of the Fortune 50") at +22% vs control, versus logo strips at +8% and aggregate stats ("Trusted by 12,400") at +9%.

**Why this beats logos alone:** Specificity. "12 Olympic training facilities" beats "Trusted by leading sports facilities." Names tell the visitor the buyer profile they recognize.

**Per vertical (variable):**
- The named-customer count and the type of named customers
- Optional: 3 to 5 logos secondary, only if recognizable in-vertical

**Constant:**
- Position (immediately below hero)
- Format: one bold count statement, optional logo strip below

**Atmos copy direction:**
- Equine: "Used by 11 equine vet clinics and 4 thoroughbred training facilities across the West."
- Clinics: "Trusted by 38 chiropractic and PT clinics across San Diego County."
- Sports Recovery: "Equipment in 6 college athletic departments and 9 pro training facilities."
- Wellness/Medspa: "Installed in 22 medspas and wellness studios from San Diego to LA."

*(Replace with real numbers from your install base. If you do not have these yet, name 3 to 5 specific facilities by name rather than aggregating. "Built for clinics like [X], [Y], and [Z]" outperforms a fake-feeling count.)*

---

### Module 3: Loss-framed problem

**Job:** Anchor the buyer in what they are losing today by not offering localized cryotherapy. Loss framing beats gain framing for high-consideration B2B (Genesys Growth 2026, Directive 2026).

**Pattern:** 1 to 2 sentence problem statement. Numbers over adjectives.

**Why this slot exists:** B2B buyers initiate first contact 70% through their journey (Demand Gen 2020). The page has to surface the buyer's existing pain in their language before introducing your solution, or the next module reads as a feature dump.

**Per vertical (variable):**
- The pain
- The numbers around the pain (cost of inaction)

**Constant:**
- Position (after social proof, before solution)
- Format: short, declarative, peer-to-peer tone

**Atmos copy direction:**
- Equine: "Every horse you send out for cryo is a referral fee you pay, a trailer trip you schedule, and a recovery window you do not control."
- Clinics: "Patients ask about cryotherapy. They book it somewhere else. That is a $4K to $12K monthly revenue line walking out your front door."
- Sports Recovery: "Athletes need recovery faster than your current setup delivers. Slow recovery is missed training blocks and shorter careers."
- Wellness/Medspa: "Cryotherapy is one of the top three searched wellness services in your zip code. You are sending that traffic to your competitor."

---

### Module 4: Three-pillar solution (Equipment, Training, Support)

**Job:** Translate Atmos's core differentiator (Equipment + Training + Support) into the vertical's workflow. Buyers in different verticals weigh these pillars differently. Sports facilities care about training protocols. Vets care about equipment specs. Medspas care about ongoing support.

**Pattern:** Three columns or three stacked cards. Each pillar = 1 outcome statement + 1 supporting detail. Outcome-focused headlines beat feature-focused (Directive 2026).

**Why this works for Atmos specifically:** Your three-pillar offer is the differentiator. Competitors sell equipment. You sell a full operating model. Every vertical page has to make this concrete in their language.

**Per vertical (variable):**
- Outcome statement under each pillar
- Supporting detail under each pillar

**Constant:**
- The three pillars (Equipment, Training, Support)
- Layout, iconography, position

**Atmos copy direction (Clinics example):**
- Equipment: "FDA-registered units sized for treatment rooms under 200 sq ft." Detail: "Plug-and-play install in 4 hours."
- Training: "Your PTs and chiropractors trained to deliver protocols in 2 days." Detail: "Certification included. Refresher training quarterly."
- Support: "Local San Diego support team on a 4-hour response SLA." Detail: "Loaner units shipped same-day if yours is ever down."

---

### Module 5: Use cases / protocols

**Job:** Show the buyer "this is how my facility uses this." Industrial buyers scan for specific data points rather than reading linearly (Lovable 2026). They need to see the concrete application before they will book.

**Pattern:** 3 to 4 vertical-specific scenarios. Each scenario = title + 2 to 3 sentence description + optional supporting visual.

**Per vertical (variable):**
- The actual use cases
- The conditions, treatments, protocols named

**Constant:**
- 3 to 4 cards (not more, not fewer)
- Format and position

**Atmos copy direction (Equine example):**
- Post-workout recovery: "Reduce inflammation in 18 minutes after a hard session. Built into your existing turnout schedule."
- Soft tissue injury: "Localized cold therapy on tendons and joints. Protocols developed with [named vet partner]."
- Pre-event prep: "Tighten and recover the night before competition. Mobile cart option included."
- Geriatric maintenance: "Standing protocols for older performance horses. 12-minute sessions, twice weekly."

---

### Module 6: Anchored testimonial

**Job:** One face. One name. One title. One facility. The Digital Applied study found single testimonial cards with face/name/title at +14% vs control, beating walls of testimonials and aggregate ratings.

**Pattern:** One large testimonial card. Face photo, name, title, facility name, 2 to 3 sentence quote. Quote anchored to a specific business outcome with a number.

**Why one beats many:** Walls of testimonials suggest you are trying to convince. One named, specific testimonial with an outcome reads as evidence. Nielsen research shows 83% of buyers trust peer recommendations (Genesys 2026).

**Per vertical (variable):**
- The testimonial (must be from same vertical as the page)
- The outcome number quoted

**Constant:**
- Format, position (near the second CTA), one testimonial only

**Atmos copy direction:** Lead each quote with a number, end with a peer-language line. Example for Clinics: "We added 47 new patients in the first 90 days. The training meant my staff could deliver protocols week one. The support team in San Diego picks up the phone." Dr. Sarah K., DC, [Clinic Name], San Diego.

---

### Module 7: Visual proof (equipment in real environment)

**Job:** Show the equipment working in the same vertical the buyer operates in. Generic stock imagery creates immediate credibility gaps (Lovable 2026). 67% of B2B buyers rely on content more than the prior year (Demand Gen 2020).

**Pattern:** Short video loop (3 to 5 seconds) or annotated photo. Not staged. Real facility. Real practitioner. Real patient/horse/athlete/client.

**Why short loops beat long videos:** Long videos hurt LCP and require commitment. 3 to 5 second loops show motion, prove it is real, and load fast.

**Per vertical (variable):**
- The footage itself
- Optional annotations calling out spec details

**Constant:**
- Format (loop or annotated photo)
- Length budget (under 5 seconds for video, under 200KB for image)

**Implementation note:** This is the module that requires the most production investment. Worth it. If you only get one vertical's footage done in v1, do equine first (highest visual differentiation, hardest to fake with stock).

---

### Module 8: Objection-handling FAQ

**Job:** Address the last-mile risk that keeps the buyer from booking. Trust signals near CTAs drive 34 to 42% conversion lifts (Surface Labs 2025).

**Pattern:** 5 to 7 vertical-specific objections in accordion or expanded list. Each Q reduces a specific risk: compliance, certifications, warranty, support response time, training duration, financing, install time.

**Per vertical (variable):**
- The specific objections (compliance varies wildly: equine has different regulations than medspa)

**Constant:**
- Format, position (before the final CTA), 5 to 7 questions

**Atmos copy direction (Clinics example questions):**
- Is the equipment FDA-registered? Yes. We carry FDA Class II registration.
- What does install actually look like? 4 hours, plug-and-play, no facility modifications.
- How long until my staff is delivering protocols? 2 days for certification. Patients on day 3.
- What if the unit goes down? 4-hour response SLA in San Diego County. Loaner shipped same-day.
- What about financing? 36-month financing available. Monthly payment under $1,200 on most configurations.
- Do I need a license? No additional license required for chiropractic or PT clinics in California.
- What does support cost annually? Included in year one. Optional service plan year two and beyond.

---

### Module 9: Trust signals adjacent to the CTA

**Job:** Stack credibility at the conversion moment. Place trust badges within view of every major CTA on the page (Intuitia 2026).

**Pattern:** Compliance badges, warranty terms, support guarantees, financing options. Microcopy at the form. Not a separate "trust section." Live next to each CTA.

**Per vertical (variable):**
- Which compliance badges apply (HIPAA for clinics, USDA-relevant standards for equine, etc.)
- The financing or warranty language

**Constant:**
- Position (adjacent to every CTA, not just the final one)
- Format (small badge + 1 line of microcopy)

**Atmos copy direction (microcopy near form):**
- "FDA-registered equipment."
- "36-month financing available."
- "4-hour support response SLA in San Diego County."
- "No-cost onsite training included."

---

### Module 10: Final CTA + form

**Job:** Capture the lead with minimum friction. The Digital Applied study found 1-field forms at 12.4% conversion, 6+ field forms at 3.1%. Lovable found 3 to 5 fields the standard for B2B equipment, extending to 4 to 6 for higher complexity.

**Pattern:**
- Single CTA verb matched to buying mode ("Book a demo" or "Get a quote")
- 3 fields maximum: name, work email, facility name
- Optional fourth: facility type (drop-down with 4 vertical options) for routing
- Trust microcopy directly below button
- No phone number required (lowers conversion)
- No checkboxes for marketing opt-ins (use double-opt-in email instead)

**Why 3 fields:** Each field past 4 roughly halves conversion (Digital Applied 2026). For Atmos, name + email + facility name is the minimum viable qualification set. Everything else can be enriched post-submission with Clearbit or ZoomInfo or asked on the discovery call.

**Per vertical (variable):**
- The CTA verb (could be "Book a demo" for some verticals, "Get a quote" for others)
- The microcopy

**Constant:**
- Field count (3, max 4)
- Single CTA
- Position (above the fold on mobile after final scroll, sticky on desktop)

**Atmos copy direction:**
- Button: "Book a demo" or "Get a quote"
- Microcopy below: "15-minute call. We will send a quote within 24 hours."

## Sticky-bottom CTA (persistent across all modules)

Not a module. A persistent UI element that runs across the entire page.

**Pattern:** Sticky-bottom bar visible on scroll throughout. Sticky-bottom CTAs alone lift conversion +11% (Digital Applied 2026).

**Mobile behavior:** Sticky bar visible at all times below the fold. On desktop, optional sticky navbar CTA (Genesys 2026 found these "consistently winning").

**Copy:** Same verb as the hero CTA. Same single action.

## Page-level requirements (non-negotiable)

1. **LCP under 2s.** Compress hero images. No video autoplay in hero. Preload critical fonts. Pages over 4s LCP convert at 1.7% vs 4.4% under 1s.

2. **No top nav on paid traffic destinations.** Or muted nav with logo only. Removing nav increases conversion 2 to 3x (GrowthSpree 2026). If you must keep nav, no dropdowns, no secondary CTAs.

3. **Single primary CTA across the page.** Same verb. Same destination. No competing actions like "Download brochure," "Sign up for newsletter," "Watch webinar."

4. **Mobile-first build.** 68% of B2B research happens on mobile. Desktop is the secondary canvas.

5. **Speed-to-lead under 5 minutes.** Leads contacted within 5 minutes convert at 3x the rate of 24+ hour follow-up (Surface Labs 2025). This is an ops requirement, not a page requirement, but the page is wasted without it. Recommend automated calendar booking via Calendly or Chili Piper directly on the page so the demo is on the calendar before the form even submits.

6. **Distributed trust.** Place a trust signal within view of every CTA. Not one stacked social-proof section.

## Sequence rationale

The 10-module sequence maps to the standard B2B buyer cognitive flow: relevance (hero) → social proof (named bar) → problem awareness (loss-framed) → solution (3 pillars) → application (use cases) → validation (testimonial) → tangible proof (visual) → objection handling (FAQ) → trust at conversion (signals near CTA) → action (final form).

This is the "role context, outcome, mechanism, confidence cue, next step" framework Unicornplatform 2026 found across high-converting B2B pages, expanded to handle the longer industrial sales cycle and multi-stakeholder buying group.

## Tradeoffs to be honest about

1. **Industrial baseline is 1.5 to 2.2%.** A 4% conversion rate on Atmos vertical pages would be excellent. Do not benchmark against B2B SaaS averages.

2. **The 3-field form leaves qualification on the table.** You will get some unqualified leads. The tradeoff is worth it because each field past 4 cuts your volume roughly in half. Qualify on the discovery call instead.

3. **Single-testimonial module means one buyer voice per page.** If you have multiple strong testimonials per vertical, save them for the case study deep-dive page. The vertical landing page is not the place.

4. **Per-vertical content is real work.** Hero copy, image, social proof count, problem language, 3-pillar copy, use cases, testimonial, FAQ, proof imagery. Roughly 30 to 40 content items per vertical. Plan production accordingly.

5. **Custom B2B pages convert 11.6% vs 3.8% for templates** (Digital Applied 2026). The modular template approach is a compromise. You are buying speed-to-market and consistency at the cost of some peak conversion. Mitigate by spending the saved time on per-vertical content quality, not on building four separate page systems.

## Measurement (what to track per page)

- Visitor to demo-booked conversion rate (primary)
- Cost per demo booked from paid traffic
- Demo-to-quote-sent rate (downstream, but the page influences it via qualification quality)
- Quote-to-close rate (downstream)
- LCP (technical health)
- Form abandonment rate by field (diagnoses friction)
- Scroll depth to FAQ and final CTA (diagnoses module sequence)

Targets for v1:
- Visitor → demo booked: 3 to 5%
- LCP: under 2s on 4G mobile
- Form abandonment: under 30%
- Scroll-to-FAQ: above 40%

## Build order recommendation

1. **Build the design system first.** Components, type, color, sticky CTA, form, FAQ accordion, testimonial card. Once. Used by all four pages.

2. **Build one vertical end-to-end as the proof.** Recommend starting with Clinics & Chiropractic. Highest existing demand awareness, clearest ROI math for the buyer (revenue per patient), easiest to write testimonials for.

3. **Validate with paid traffic for 2 weeks.** Run small budget ($500 to $1K) into the live page. Measure visitor → demo conversion. If you hit 3% on cold traffic, the template works.

4. **Roll out the remaining three verticals.** Each subsequent page is roughly 25 to 35% of the effort of the first one because the design system, components, and architecture are already built.

5. **A/B test the highest-leverage modules.** Hero H1, named social proof count, CTA verb. Run one test at a time. Wait for statistical significance (1,000+ sessions per variant minimum).
