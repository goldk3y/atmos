# The Science of Cryotherapy & Localized Cryotherapy — A Comprehensive Evidence Dossier for Atmos / CryoGun

## TL;DR
- Localized handheld cryotherapy is supported by a deep mechanistic literature (vasoconstriction, nerve-conduction slowing, IL-6/IL-1β/TNF-α downregulation, norepinephrine release, collagen stimulation) and by RCT-level evidence for post-surgical pain, tendinopathy, knee osteoarthritis, exercise-induced soreness, and cryolipolysis-style fat reduction.
- The strongest scientifically defensible claims center on: pain reduction, inflammation reduction, faster recovery from exercise/injury, improved local circulation, post-operative swelling and opioid reduction, and skin/aesthetic effects (collagen, microcirculation, puffiness).
- Weaker or speculative claims — cure of disease (Alzheimer's, MS, asthma), "burns 500–800 kcal per session," depression treatment from a single handheld device — should be avoided or carefully hedged because the FDA has explicitly warned that whole-body cryotherapy is not approved to treat any medical condition.

---

## Key Findings

1. **Mechanism is settled science.** Cryotherapy works through five well-documented physiological pathways: (1) sympathetic α-adrenergic vasoconstriction followed by reactive vasodilation (the Lewis hunting response, first described in *Heart* 1930), (2) reduced peripheral nerve conduction velocity producing analgesia (Algafly & George 2007 — 32.8% NCV reduction, 89% pain-threshold increase), (3) suppression of pro-inflammatory cytokines IL-6, IL-1β and TNF-α at the gene and protein level (Guillot 2017; He et al. 2025 *Sci Rep*), (4) catecholamine release (norepinephrine +200–530%, dopamine +250%; Šrámek 2000, Leppäluoto 2008), and (5) brown adipose tissue activation increasing resting metabolic rate (van Marken Lichtenbelt, NEJM 2009; Blondin et al. 2014).

2. **The strongest clinical evidence is for pain and post-surgical recovery.** The 2024 Liang et al. meta-analysis of 31 RCTs (*Orthopaedic Surgery*) showed cryotherapy after total knee arthroplasty significantly reduced VAS pain on days 1–3 and cut opioid consumption. The 2025 Bechir meta-analysis of knee OA RCTs showed SMD −0.57 (p = 0.004) favoring cryotherapy plus exercise.

3. **DOMS evidence is real but modality-specific.** Zhang et al. (*Front Sports Act Living* 2026; 51 RCTs, n=1,243) showed local cold therapy produced the greatest DOMS reduction at 48 h (MD = −1.17), while whole-body cryotherapy produced the largest creatine-kinase reductions at 48–72 h and counter-movement-jump improvements through 48 h; no modality reduced DOMS immediately post-exercise.

4. **Localized cryotherapy specifically is supported** for tennis elbow (Kawa 2015 RCT), Achilles tendon microcirculation (Knobloch 2008 RCT, n=60), plantar fasciitis (Costantino 2014 RCT, n=102), and rheumatoid synovial inflammation (Peyronnel/Guillot studies).

5. **Aesthetic and recovery uses are the safest commercial territory.** Collagen stimulation, microcirculation flush, puffiness reduction, pore contraction, post-workout soreness, and acute-injury swelling are mechanistically clean claims that avoid FDA medical-device territory.

6. **Professional sports adoption is genuine and well-documented.** At least 15 NFL teams use cryotherapy, the UFC Performance Institute has dedicated chambers, and named users include LeBron James, Cristiano Ronaldo, Stephen Curry, Floyd Mayweather and the 2011 championship Dallas Mavericks.

## Details

### 1. Foundations: What Cryotherapy Is and Where It Came From

**Definition.** Cryotherapy (from Greek *kryos*, "icy cold") is the therapeutic application of cold — by contact ice, cold water immersion, refrigerated air, vaporized nitrogen, or pressurized cold CO₂ — to elicit specific physiological responses: vasoconstriction, slowing of nerve conduction, suppression of inflammatory cytokines, and reflex catecholamine release.

**Origins.** Cold-as-medicine has been documented since the Edwin Smith Papyrus (~2500 BCE Egypt) and was systematized by Hippocrates. The modern era of clinical cryotherapy begins in 1845 with English physician James Arnott, who used salt-ice mixtures to treat tumors and neuralgia. The pivotal moment for systemic cryotherapy came in **1978, when Japanese rheumatologist Dr. Toshima Yamaguchi** in Ōita built the first whole-body cryogenic chamber and began treating rheumatoid arthritis patients with brief exposures to ultra-low temperatures, discovering that very rapid skin cooling produced a far more potent analgesic and endorphin-releasing effect than slow cooling in an ice bath. His work was carried into Europe in the early 1980s by Reinhard Fricke in Germany and later refined for sports recovery in Poland.

**Modern taxonomy.**

| Modality | Typical agent | Temperature | Duration | Coverage |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Whole-Body Cryotherapy (WBC) | Liquid-N₂ vapor or electric chamber | −110 °C to −160 °C (−166 °F to −256 °F) | 2–4 min | Whole body, head out (cryosauna) or head in (chamber) |
| Partial-Body Cryotherapy (PBC) | N₂ cryosauna | −110 °C to −140 °C | 2–3 min | Neck-down |
| **Localized cryotherapy** | Pressurized CO₂ (−78 °C source) or cold air | Skin cooled to ~4 °C; emitted gas −78 °C to −110 °F | 30 s–4 min per area | Targeted (joint, face, tendon) |
| Cold-water immersion (CWI) / ice bath | Water | 5–15 °C | 10–20 min | Body or limb |
| Ice pack / cryocuff | Frozen gel/water | 0 °C | 15–20 min | Local |
| Cryo facial | Cold air or CO₂ | −78 °C source | 5–12 min | Face/neck |
| Cryolipolysis | Vacuum-coupled cold plate | −5 °C to −11 °C tissue | 35–60 min | Adipose pocket |
| Cryosurgery / cryoneurolysis | Liquid N₂ or N₂O probe | −20 °C to −196 °C | Variable | Lesion, nerve |

Localized cryotherapy occupies the practical middle ground: deeper and more intense than ice packs (no insulating water layer; very low source temperature; high-velocity jet creates micro-massage and rapid heat extraction), but more precise, lower-risk, and dramatically more accessible than whole-body chambers.

### 2. How a Handheld Cryotherapy Device (CryoGun) Works

Commercial handheld cryotherapy devices (e.g., the CryoGun, Cryo Innovations XR, InstantCryo, SubZero) use **pressurized liquid CO₂** at roughly 800 psi from a standard 20-lb cylinder. The CO₂ passes through a **Joule-Thomson nozzle** that throttles the gas and induces an isenthalpic expansion, producing a jet of solid-and-gaseous CO₂ at approximately **−78 °C (−108 °F)** with high velocity (US Patent 12,256,970 describes this exact architecture).

The jet does three things simultaneously:
1. **Rapidly extracts heat** from a small skin patch, dropping surface temperature from ~32 °C to ~4 °C in 30 seconds.
2. **Creates a mechanical micro-massage** from the pressurized stream that drives the cold deeper into tissue than passive ice and stimulates mechanoreceptors.
3. **Triggers a "thermal shock" reflex** — the rapid rate of change (rather than absolute temperature) is what maximally activates cutaneous thermoreceptors and sympathetic outflow.

Compared to a traditional ice bag, this produces a steeper thermal gradient, deeper cooling penetration over a shorter time, no skin-burn risk from prolonged contact ice, and far better repeatability.

### 3. Physiological Mechanisms of Action

**3.1 Vasoconstriction → reactive vasodilation ("Lewis hunting reaction").** Cold first stimulates α-adrenergic receptors and the sympathetic nervous system, contracting cutaneous arterioles and shutting down local blood flow. After 5–10 minutes of sustained cold, **Sir Thomas Lewis (1930, *Heart* 15:177–208)** described an oscillating vasodilation in distal extremities — the *hunting response* — in which arteriovenous anastomoses suddenly open to flush warm blood through tissue, then close again, producing a 3–5 °C rebound in skin temperature. This protective cycle delivers a wave of oxygenated blood and immune cells to the cooled area when treatment ends — the "flush" that clinicians describe after a cryotherapy session. (See also Daanen HAM, *Eur J Appl Physiol* 89:411-26, 2003.)

**3.2 Slowed nerve conduction velocity (NCV) → analgesia.** **Algafly & George (2007, *Br J Sports Med* 41:365-9)** showed that local ankle cryotherapy cooling skin to 10 °C produced a **32.8% reduction in tibial-nerve conduction velocity**, with a corresponding **89% rise in pressure-pain threshold at the iced site** and a **76% rise in pain tolerance**. Even at a distal non-iced site served by the same nerve, threshold rose 71% and tolerance 56%. This is the strongest single-paper mechanistic basis for cryotherapy's analgesic effect.

**3.3 Suppression of inflammatory cytokines (IL-6, IL-1β, TNF-α, NF-κB).**
- **Guillot et al. (2017, PMC5536266)** showed in an arthritic-rat model that twice-daily local cryotherapy (ice and cold gas) significantly downregulated IL-6, IL-17A and IL-1β at the gene and protein levels in hind paws, and reduced plasma IL-17A.
- **Peyronnel et al. (PMC9052534)** showed cryotherapy reduced aortic mRNA expression of CXCL-1, IL-6, IL-1β and TNF-α and improved endothelial function in arthritic rats.
- **Klemm et al. (2022, *Clin Exp Rheumatol* 40:2133-40)** RCT in 56 rheumatoid-arthritis patients found WBC (6 sessions over 14 days at −130 °C × 3 min) significantly reduced pain (between-group difference −1.31 NRS, p = 0.002), disease activity, and TNF/IL-6 levels, with 58% reducing or discontinuing analgesics at 12 weeks.
- **He, Zhang, Ge et al. (2025, *Sci Rep* 15:7759; doi:10.1038/s41598-025-90396-3; 11 RCTs, total n = 274)** found WBC significantly lowered IL-1β (SMD −2.08 pg/mL, p < 0.05) and raised anti-inflammatory IL-10 (SMD +0.78 pg/mL, p < 0.05); athletes benefited most on IL-1β reduction.
- **Guilhem et al. (2013, *Sci Rep*)** in rat muscle showed local cryotherapy reduced TNF-α, NF-κB, TGF-β and MMP-9 expression and macrophage infiltration without impairing regeneration.

**3.4 Norepinephrine, dopamine and endogenous opioid release.**
- **Šrámek et al. (2000, *Eur J Appl Physiol* 81:436-42; PMID 10751106):** 1 h cold-water immersion at 14 °C raised plasma **noradrenaline by 530%** and **dopamine by 250%**.
- **Leppäluoto et al. (2008, *Scand J Clin Lab Invest* 68:145-53):** 3-month WBC programs in healthy women elevated norepinephrine 200–300% per session, an effect that did not habituate over 12 weeks.
- These catecholamine surges underlie cold's documented effects on alertness, mood, and pain perception.

**3.5 Brown adipose tissue (BAT) activation.** **van Marken Lichtenbelt et al. (2009, *NEJM* 360:1500-8)** — 23 of 24 (96%) healthy adult men showed metabolically active BAT under cold exposure (16 °C) that was undetectable at thermoneutrality. BAT activity was significantly lower in overweight/obese versus lean men (p = 0.007) and correlated positively with resting metabolic rate (r = 0.64). **Blondin et al. (PMC4213359)** showed 4 weeks of cold acclimation produced a **45% increase in BAT volume** and a **2.2-fold increase in cold-induced BAT oxidative metabolism**. **Chondronikola et al. (2014, *Diabetes* 63:4089–4099)** reported that prolonged mild cold exposure (19 °C for 5–8 h) increased resting energy expenditure by 15% in BAT-positive individuals.

**3.6 Endorphin release & HPA-axis modulation.** Rapid skin cooling triggers cutaneous cold-receptor firing that reflexively elevates β-endorphin and ACTH — the original observation from Yamaguchi's WBC patients and replicated in healthy-female long-term cold studies (Leppäluoto 2008).

**3.7 Collagen and skin effects.** Cold-induced vasoconstriction followed by rebound vasodilation increases delivery of oxygen and nutrients to the dermis. The thermal stress also activates fibroblasts. Cold-activated TRPM8 channels on keratinocytes upregulate the expression of structural proteins. Clinical reviews in cosmetic dermatology (e.g., *J Clin Aesthet Dermatol* 2020) describe consistent short-term firming and pore-tightening, with longer-term collagen stimulation observed after repeated sessions (Tlish et al. 2018).

### 4. Clinical Applications — Evidence Tier by Tier

#### 4.1 Athletic Recovery & Performance

**DOMS / exercise-induced muscle damage.** **Zhang et al. (*Front Sports Act Living* 8:1819396, 2026; 51 RCTs, n = 1,243)** found modality-specific time-dependent effects: WBC produced the largest CK reductions at 48 h (MD = −118.24 U/L, p < 0.05) and 72 h (MD = −135.03), and improved CMJ at 1 h (MD = +9.15 cm), 24 h (+10.70 cm), and 48 h (+10.50 cm); **local cold therapy (LCT) produced the greatest DOMS reduction at 48 h (MD = −1.17)**; no modality significantly reduced DOMS immediately post-exercise. **Dupuy et al. (2018, *Frontiers in Physiology*)** meta-analysis of 99 studies found cryotherapy produces small-to-large reductions in DOMS (Hedges' *g* −2.26 to −0.40). **Hohenauer et al. (2015, *PLoS One*)** systematic review of 36 articles confirmed cooling reduces subjective DOMS/RPE and blood-plasma CK and cytokines vs passive recovery.

A specific caveat on *local* cryotherapy for DOMS: **Costa et al. (2019, *Sport Sciences for Health*; 7 RCTs)** found local contact cryotherapy alone did not statistically accelerate DOMS recovery vs control in their pooled estimate, so the strongest DOMS-related claim from a handheld device should center on perceived soreness, circulation flush, and combined-modality recovery rather than promising a fixed percentage reduction.

**Performance / return to play.** WBC and CWI both produce small but consistent improvements in next-day countermovement-jump performance and perceived recovery in trained athletes (Zhang 2026; Higgins 2017 *J Strength Cond Res*).

#### 4.2 Pain Management

**Chronic pain (general).** **Garcia, Karri, Zacharias, Abd-Elsayed (2021, *Pain Ther* 10:81-100; doi:10.1007/s40122-020-00225-w)** — the most cited modern narrative review — concluded "Both local and non-local cryotherapy applications show promise in reducing chronic pain associated with various chronic diseases including those of rheumatic and degenerative origin."

**Chronic low back pain.** **Nugraha et al. (2015, *Eur J Phys Rehabil Med* 51:143-8; PMID 25296740)** — RCT showed a 2-week WBC protocol significantly improved pain perception in chronic LBP versus sham. **Pawik et al. (2022, *J Orthop Sci* 27:1192-7)** — 41-patient trial of 20 cryo sessions produced significant reductions in PNRS, Oswestry Disability Index, Roland-Morris (all p < 0.001) and reduced IL-2 while raising IL-10 (p = 0.003). Both used whole-body chambers; handheld-device claims for LBP should be mechanism-based (NCV slowing, IL-6/IL-1β downregulation).

**Tennis elbow (lateral epicondylitis).** **Kawa & Kowza-Dzwonkowska (2015, *Balt J Health Phys Act* 7(3):73-87)** RCT in 34 patients: 10 local cryotherapy sessions over 2 weeks produced significant reductions in pain intensity (VAS, Laitinen) and analgesic use vs control. A 2022 non-randomized clinical study (PMC9140546) using sEMG documented direct effects of local cryotherapy on muscle stimulation, pain and grip strength.

**Plantar fasciitis.** **Costantino et al. (2014, *Eur J Phys Rehabil Med* 50:39-47; PMID 24172641)** — RCT n = 102 chronic plantar fasciitis patients: 10 sessions of cryoultrasound vs cryotherapy. Between-group VAS difference of 4.35 points at 12 months (95% CI 3.75–4.95; p < 0.001) favoring cryoultrasound; both arms improved significantly.

**Achilles tendinopathy.** **Knobloch et al. (2008, *Am J Sports Med* 36:2128-38; doi:10.1177/0363546508319313)** RCT n = 60: intermittent localized cryotherapy significantly reduced superficial and deep capillary blood flow within the mid-portion Achilles tendon (p = 0.0001). Romero-Morales et al. (2019, *Int Wound J* 16:542-9) RCT n = 40 found localized cryotherapy added to eccentric exercise produced ultrasonographic improvements in tendon thickness/CSA in chronic tendinopathy.

**Knee osteoarthritis.** Bechir et al. systematic review with meta-analysis (PMC12168428; 5 RCTs) — SMD for pain −0.57 (95% CI −0.97 to −0.18; p = 0.004) favoring cryotherapy + exercise vs exercise alone.

**Post-surgical / total knee arthroplasty.** **Liang et al. (2024, *Orthop Surg* 16:2925-42; doi:10.1111/os.14266)** — meta-analysis of 31 RCTs: cryotherapy after TKA significantly reduced post-op VAS pain on days 1, 2, 3 (MDs −0.59, −0.84, −0.86; all p < 0.05), reduced opioid consumption, hemoglobin loss, drainage, and improved ROM. **Aoyagi et al. (2023, *J Arthroplasty* 38:988-94; doi:10.1016/j.arth.2022.11.018)** — significantly less opioid use in the first post-op week (p < 0.05), with stronger effects from temperature-regulated computer-assisted cryotherapy than from traditional ice packs.

**Migraine.** **Sprouse-Blum et al. (2013, *Hawaii J Med Public Health* 72(7):237; PMC3727573)** — randomized crossover RCT n = 55: targeted neck cooling at the carotid arteries produced a **31.8 ± 15.2% pain reduction at 30 minutes vs a 31.5 ± 20% pain increase in the control arm**. **Ucler et al. (2006, *Evid Based Complement Alternat Med* 3:489-93)** open-label cold-cap therapy reduced migraine pain in pilot study.

**Carpal tunnel and nerve pain.** Cryoneurolysis (probe-based) has robust evidence; for non-invasive surface cryotherapy, claims should be analgesic and inflammatory based on the Algafly NCV mechanism.

#### 4.3 Inflammation & Acute Injury

The PRICE/POLICE protocol (Protection, Optimal Loading, Ice, Compression, Elevation) is universally endorsed in sports-medicine guidelines for acute sprains, strains and contusions. Mechanism: cold reduces capillary permeability, secondary hypoxic tissue death, and edema. **Bleakley et al. (2014, *Open Access J Sports Med* 5:25-36)** is the canonical narrative review.

#### 4.4 Joint & Musculoskeletal Conditions

**Rheumatoid arthritis.** **Klemm 2022** RCT (above); **Hirvonen et al. (2006, *Clin Exp Rheumatol* 24:295-301)** — randomized comparison of WBC −110 °C, cold-pack and local cold air found pain reduced most markedly in the WBC arm; **Gizińska et al. (2015, *Biomed Res Int* 2015:409174)** showed both WBC and traditional rehab reduced IL-6 and TNF-α significantly. The 2025 comprehensive review (PMC12744633) explicitly states cryotherapy "significantly reduced the white blood cell count and inflammatory cytokine concentration in the synovial fluid."

**Ankylosing spondylitis.** 2025 meta-analysis of 5 studies (310 patients): WBC significantly improved BASDAI (p < 0.001), ASDAS (p = 0.015), BASFI (p = 0.006) and VAS pain (p = 0.005). **Straburzyńska-Lupa et al. (2018, PMC6192087)** documented reductions in pro-inflammatory cytokines.

**Fibromyalgia.** **Vitenet et al. (2018, *Complement Ther Med* 36:6-8)** RCT n = 24: 10 WBC sessions significantly improved health-related quality of life vs control, lasting ≥1 month. **Klemm et al. (2021, *Adv Rheumatol* 61:3)** showed cytokine profile alteration with serial WBC in FM. **Varallo et al. (2022, *J Clin Med* 11:4324)** RCT n = 43: WBC add-on to rehab produced greater improvements in pain severity, depressive symptoms, disease impact and sleep quality.

**Multiple sclerosis.** **Miller et al. (2016, *Acta Neurol Scand* 134:420-6)** case-control: WBC improved fatigue and functional status in MS patients. The FDA explicitly cited MS in its 2016 warning against unproven cryotherapy claims, so marketing should hedge.

#### 4.5 Skin & Beauty

**Collagen, microcirculation, pore tightening.** A 2020 *J Clin Aesthet Dermatol* review concluded cryotherapy for skin health shows "promising results" for rejuvenation and inflammation reduction, while noting that large-scale long-term trials remain limited. Mechanism: cold-induced vasoconstriction-rebound cycle increases dermal blood flow and oxygen delivery; fibroblast cold-stress activation upregulates type-I/III collagen synthesis; pore size visibly decreases acutely from contraction.

**Eczema & psoriasis.** **Lee et al. (2021)** showed localized cryotherapy reduced itching in mild-to-moderate atopic dermatitis immediately post-treatment and at 1, 2, and 8 weeks. **Shamsadini et al. (2005)** found localized cryotherapy improved or resolved psoriatic plaques. **Tlish et al. (2018)** found maximal effect after 10 sessions.

**Anti-puffiness / under-eye / facial lifting.** Acute vasoconstriction physically reduces edema and visible puffiness; this is the immediate, observable, and uncontested benefit of cryo-facials.

**Hair loss / scalp.** Mechanism evidence only — increased scalp microcirculation post-cryotherapy may support follicular nutrient delivery — no RCT-level evidence yet.

#### 4.6 Weight Loss & Fat Reduction

**Cryolipolysis.** FDA-cleared (CoolSculpting) for noninvasive fat reduction starting 2010 (flanks) and 2012 (abdomen). Mechanism: adipocytes are more susceptible to cold-induced apoptosis than skin/nerves/muscle (Manstein 2008). **Ingargiola et al. (2015, *Plast Reconstr Surg* 135:1581-90; doi:10.1097/PRS.0000000000001236)** — systematic review of 19 studies: **14.67–28.5% reduction in fat-layer thickness by caliper, 10.3–25.5% by ultrasound** per treatment cycle. Garibyan et al. (2014, *Lasers Surg Med* 46:75-80) reported ~39 cm³ mean absolute volume loss per treatment by 3D imaging.

**Critical note:** True cryolipolysis requires sustained −5 to −11 °C tissue temperature for 35–60 minutes with vacuum coupling. A handheld 30-second CO₂ spray cannot replicate this and should not be marketed as cryolipolysis. The legitimate claim is local microcirculation, contour-tightening via skin effects, and BAT-region stimulation.

**BAT activation.** Van Marken Lichtenbelt 2009 and Blondin 2014 as cited above; Chondronikola 2014 showed 15% resting-energy-expenditure increase in BAT-positive individuals.

#### 4.7 Mental Health & Mood

**Depression.** **Rymaszewska et al. (2020, *Front Psychiatry* 11:522)** — randomized sham-controlled trial n = 92 adults with depressive episode: 10 WBC sessions as add-on to pharmacotherapy significantly improved HAM-D and BDI-II scores and quality of life vs sham. Earlier work (Rymaszewska et al. 2008, PMC2734249) showed 34.6% of WBC patients vs 2.9% controls achieved ≥50% reduction in HAM-D after 3 weeks.

**Anxiety.** Same Rymaszewska cohort: 46.2% of WBC patients vs 0% of controls achieved ≥50% HARS reduction.

**Sleep.** Bouzigon et al. work on single-session WBC has reported next-day sleep quality and energy improvements in active men.

These trials are whole-body. A handheld device's plausible mental-health effect derives from the norepinephrine surge (Šrámek 2000; Leppäluoto 2008) when sufficient skin area is treated. Avoid medical-treatment language; use wellness/mood-support language.

#### 4.8 Other

**Immune system.** Repeated WBC has been associated with increased lymphocyte and NK cell counts in healthy subjects and modulated leukocyte profile in athletes (Lombardi et al. 2017, *Front Physiol*).

**Lymphatic drainage / circulation.** Mechanism: the vasoconstriction–vasodilation oscillation acts as a "pump" promoting lymph and venous return.

### 5. Strength-of-Evidence Tiering for Marketing Claims

**Tier A — Strong evidence (multiple RCTs / meta-analyses / clear mechanism):**
1. Reduces pain acutely at the treated site (Algafly 2007; Garcia 2021 review).
2. Reduces inflammation at cellular level — IL-6, IL-1β, TNF-α suppression (Guillot 2017; Klemm 2022; He 2025 *Sci Rep*).
3. Reduces post-surgical pain and opioid use after orthopedic surgery (Liang 2024 meta-analysis of 31 RCTs; Aoyagi 2023).
4. Reduces swelling and edema in acute soft-tissue injury (Bleakley 2014; PRICE/POLICE protocol).
5. Reduces post-exercise muscle soreness and CK, IL-6, CRP at 24–72 h (Zhang 2026 NMA; Dupuy 2018).
6. Slows nerve conduction velocity → analgesia (Algafly 2007: 32.8% NCV reduction, 89% pain-threshold increase).
7. Triggers vasoconstriction then reactive vasodilation improving local microcirculation (Lewis 1930; Knobloch 2008).
8. Releases norepinephrine 200–530% and dopamine 250% (Šrámek 2000; Leppäluoto 2008) — supports alertness and mood-elevation claims.
9. Reduces knee osteoarthritis pain when used with exercise (2025 SR meta-analysis SMD −0.57).
10. Cryolipolysis reduces localized subcutaneous fat 15–28% per cycle (Ingargiola 2015) — only at true cryolipolysis device parameters.

**Tier B — Moderate evidence (RCTs but limited/heterogeneous):**
1. Symptom improvement in rheumatoid arthritis (Klemm 2022; Hirvonen 2006).
2. Symptom improvement in ankylosing spondylitis (2025 meta-analysis).
3. Quality-of-life improvement in fibromyalgia (Vitenet 2018; Varallo 2022).
4. Tennis elbow / lateral epicondylitis pain reduction (Kawa 2015).
5. Plantar fasciitis pain reduction with cryoultrasound combination (Costantino 2014).
6. Migraine pain reduction with targeted neck cooling (Sprouse-Blum 2013).
7. Add-on effect for depressive symptoms (Rymaszewska 2008, 2020) — whole-body context.
8. Anti-puffiness, pore-tightening, post-treatment "glow" from cryo facials.
9. Brown adipose tissue activation and 15% RMR increase (van Marken Lichtenbelt 2009; Chondronikola 2014).
10. Itch reduction in atopic dermatitis (Lee 2021); psoriasis plaque improvement (Shamsadini 2005).

**Tier C — Limited / preliminary / mechanism-only (use cautiously):**
1. Anti-aging / wrinkle reduction (mechanism plausible; large RCTs limited).
2. Cellulite reduction.
3. Hair growth / hair loss.
4. Long-term collagen remodeling.
5. Carpal tunnel relief (only via NCV mechanism; no surface-cryotherapy RCT).
6. Direct treatment of MS, Alzheimer's, asthma (FDA explicitly warned against these claims).
7. Caloric burn of "500–800 kcal per session" (popular claim with no peer-reviewed support; BAT-RMR data suggest only ~15% RMR increase, not several hundred kcal).
8. Immune system "boost" (correlational data only).
9. Anxiety/depression treatment from handheld device alone.

### 6. Comparisons

| Comparison | Verdict from evidence |
|---|---|
| Localized cryo vs ice packs | Localized cryo achieves lower source temperatures (~−78 °C vs 0 °C), faster cooling, no skin saturation, and a mechanical micro-massage. Aoyagi 2023 found temperature-regulated computer-assisted cryotherapy outperformed ice packs in TKA recovery. |
| Localized cryo vs CWI/ice bath | Hohenauer (*Scand J Med Sci Sports* 2017) found CWI and partial-body cryo produced similar 72-hour recovery outcomes despite different physiology. Localized cryo wins on accessibility, precision, and avoiding whole-body cold stress. |
| Localized cryo vs WBC | WBC has more research, especially for systemic conditions. Localized is dramatically more accessible, lower-cost, lower-risk, and allows direct targeting. Hirvonen 2006 found WBC produced the deepest pain reduction in RA, but local cold air also produced significant improvement. |
| Penetration depth | Surface skin cools to ~4 °C; deep-tissue cooling extends ~1–2 cm with 30–60 s exposure; the mechanical jet pushes cooling beyond passive ice. |

### 7. Safety & Contraindications

**Safety profile of localized cryotherapy is favorable** versus WBC. Because the user controls the wand, treats one small area at a time, and modern units include skin-temperature sensors with auto-shutoff, the asphyxiation, full-body frostbite, and eye-injury risks documented for WBC (and warned about by the FDA's 2016 Consumer Update) are largely absent. The 2023 scoping review (Bouzigon et al., PMC10537204) identified only 16 documented adverse events in published WBC literature and concluded "WBC-related safety risks are within acceptable limits and can be proactively prevented by adhering to existing recommendations, contraindications, and commonsense guidelines."

**Contraindications:** Raynaud's disease, cold urticaria/allergy, cryoglobulinemia, peripheral neuropathy, severe peripheral vascular disease, uncontrolled hypertension, recent myocardial infarction or unstable angina, deep vein thrombosis, pregnancy (precaution), open wounds, severe diabetes with neuropathy.

**Common side effects** of localized application: transient erythema, mild blanching, brief tingling, occasional skin sensitivity. Rare: superficial cold burn from too-close/too-long application (mitigated by distance sensors and auto-shutoff).

**FDA status.** The FDA has not approved any whole-body cryotherapy chamber to treat any medical condition, and explicitly warned (July 2016 Consumer Update) against claims of treating Alzheimer's, fibromyalgia, migraines, rheumatoid arthritis, MS, stress, anxiety or chronic pain. Cryosurgical / cryoablation devices are FDA-cleared for tumor/lesion ablation, and CoolSculpting (cryolipolysis) is FDA-cleared for noninvasive fat reduction in specific anatomic areas. Handheld cosmetic-use cryotherapy devices that do not make medical claims operate outside formal FDA approval but should not be marketed as treatments for disease.

### 8. Professional / Athletic Adoption

- **NFL:** At least 15 NFL teams have integrated cryotherapy. The Denver Broncos and Kansas City Chiefs maintain in-facility cryo chambers. The New Orleans Saints and Tampa Bay Buccaneers operate cryo recovery rooms. Players documented as users include Brian Orakpo, Golden Tate, Ezekiel Ansah, Tyrunn Walker, and Wallace Gilberry.
- **NBA:** LeBron James (whose business partner Maverick Carter estimated he spends ~$1.5M/year on his body; LeBron neither confirmed nor denied it in Netflix's 2024 docuseries *Starting 5*, saying "That is a number that I will not disclose, but, more importantly, I think it's just the time"), Stephen Curry, Kobe Bryant. The 2011 Dallas Mavericks championship team used cryotherapy during their playoff run.
- **Soccer:** Cristiano Ronaldo spent €45,000 (~$61,500) to install a cryotherapy chamber in his La Finca, Madrid home in 2013 (per *El Mundo* / Bleacher Report, Nov 2013; the unit was later shipped to Manchester per *The Sun*, 2021). Jamie Vardy reportedly uses home cryotherapy. Leicester City FC famously used cryotherapy during its 2015–16 Premier League title run.

## Recommendations

**Use immediately on "The Science" page:**
1. Build the page around Tier A claims, with one short paragraph per mechanism, each anchored by one named peer-reviewed source (e.g., Algafly 2007, Klemm 2022, Liang 2024, Šrámek 2000, van Marken Lichtenbelt 2009, Sprouse-Blum 2013).
2. Open with the *Br J Sports Med* nerve-conduction finding ("33% slower pain signaling") as the hero stat — it is the single most defensible, mechanism-pure, scientifically vetted figure.
3. Use Yamaguchi's 1978 origin story as the "since 1978" credibility anchor.
4. Cite the Liang 2024 meta-analysis of 31 RCTs as the strongest evidence package for pain and opioid-reduction claims.
5. For aesthetics, lead with vasoconstriction-rebound and microcirculation (uncontested), then add fibroblast/collagen language with the *J Clin Aesthet Dermatol* 2020 review as the source.

**Reframe these claims, do not remove them:**
1. DOMS / muscle recovery: frame as "supports faster perceived recovery" and "lowers IL-6, CK and CRP," not "X% faster recovery."
2. Fat reduction: discuss local circulation, microcirculation, contour effects, and BAT activation — do NOT use the word "cryolipolysis" or "CoolSculpting-equivalent."
3. Mental health: frame as "supports mood and alertness via norepinephrine release" — avoid "treats depression/anxiety."
4. Athlete endorsements: use the documented, sourced quotes (Ronaldo's €45,000 home chamber, LeBron's docuseries non-denial), not fabricated celebrity quotes.

**Remove or refuse to publish:**
1. "Burns 500–800 calories per session" — no peer-reviewed support.
2. Direct disease-treatment claims for Alzheimer's, MS, asthma, fibromyalgia, RA, or migraine.
3. Any claim that the device is "FDA-approved" — handheld cosmetic cryo devices are not.
4. Any claim of permanent fat loss from a handheld CO₂ device.

**Threshold to upgrade claims:** if a future RCT specifically tests handheld CO₂ localized cryotherapy for a condition with n > 50 and reports a positive primary outcome, that condition can move from Tier C → Tier B; an additional meta-analysis can move it to Tier A.

## Caveats
- Most large-scale clinical RCTs are on whole-body cryotherapy, cold-water immersion, or ice/contact cryotherapy — not specifically on pressurized-CO₂ handheld devices. Claims for handheld localized devices rest on (a) shared mechanism, (b) localized cryotherapy-specific studies (Kawa 2015, Knobloch 2008, Costantino 2014, Liang 2024, Guillot 2017), and (c) cosmetic/recovery outcomes rather than disease treatment.
- The FDA has explicitly warned against treatment claims for Alzheimer's, MS, fibromyalgia, migraine, rheumatoid arthritis, stress, anxiety, or chronic pain by unapproved devices.
- Several common industry claims circulate without peer-reviewed support — most notably "burns 500–800 kcal per session" and "boosts metabolism for 24 hours." Don't repeat them.
- A 30-second CO₂ jet ≠ cryolipolysis. Cryolipolysis requires sustained −5 to −11 °C tissue temperature for 35–60 minutes under vacuum coupling.
- Effect sizes for DOMS reduction vary widely across studies. Framing should be "supports faster perceived recovery" rather than "guarantees X% faster recovery."
- Mental-health claims should be wellness-framed (mood support, alertness, focus) — not treatment-framed.
- Studies cited here are primarily 2007–2026; landmark older studies (Lewis 1930, Yamaguchi 1978 work, Manstein 2008) are included for foundational mechanism and history.
- Where a study used WBC but the mechanism transfers cleanly to localized application, this report has flagged it explicitly; sales copy should do the same to maintain credibility.
